Backshift · Cambio de tiempos

Cuando el verbo introductor (said, told, explained, asked…) va en pasado, los tiempos verbales de la oración reportada retroceden un paso: present → past, past → past perfect, will → would. También cambian los pronombres y las referencias de tiempo y lugar: now → then, here → there, this → that.

🎯Linguaskill: base de la reformulación en Writing y la comprensión inferencial en Reading & Listening (entender lo que alguien dijo sobre lo que dijo otro).
🔧

C. Cuándo NO hay backshift
Excepciones — el tiempo verbal puede quedarse igual
Verdad general o hecho universal. Sin retroceso obligatorio: She said water boils at 100°C. El hecho es siempre cierto, no tiene sentido pasar al pasado.
Verbo introductor en presente o futuro. Cuando reportas con says, is saying, will say, el tiempo de la oración subordinada no cambia: He says he is tired.
La situación sigue siendo verdad. Opcional: puedes mantener el presente si la información sigue vigente: She told me she lives in Paris (and she still does).
Reporte muy reciente (justo dicho). Si reportas algo dicho hace segundos: Sorry, what did you say? — I said I am hungry.
Past perfect ya está "al fondo". No retrocede más: "I had finished by 6." → He said he had finished by 6.
Modales sin equivalente pasado. could, would, might, should, ought to, used to no tienen forma más antigua: se quedan igual.
Tiempos hipotéticos (condicionales 2 y 3). "If I had more time, I would help" → He said that if he had more time he would help. El pasado hipotético no cambia.

D. Trampas para hispanohablantes
Interferencias del español más frecuentes
!
"Say" vs "tell" — distinción que en español no existe

Ambos significan "decir", pero el patrón es distinto: say (something) [to somebody] y tell somebody (something). He said me ✗ → He told me ✓ · He told that he was tired ✗ → He said (that) he was tired ✓. Regla rápida: si va seguido directamente de la persona, es tell.

!
Backshift de will → would, can → could

No traducir literalmente "dijo que vendrá" → He said he will come ✗. En inglés es obligatorio: He said he would come. Lo mismo con can → could: She said she could swim. El español permite mantener el futuro/presente; el inglés exige el paso atrás.

!
Pronombres: cambia el deíctico, no solo el verbo

Muchos hispanohablantes hacen backshift del verbo pero olvidan el pronombre: He said that I am tired (si reportas las palabras de Juan: "I am tired"). Lo correcto: He said that he was tired. El "I" original pertenece al hablante directo, no al que reporta.

!
Referencias temporales: today ≠ today

Cuando reportas días después, today se convierte en that day, yesterday en the day before, tomorrow en the next day. He said he would come tomorrow (dicho hace una semana) es confuso → He said he would come the next day.

!
"Must" → had to (obligación), pero "must" deductivo no cambia

Para obligación: "You must leave." → He said I had to leave. Para deducción/certeza: "It must be a mistake." → She said it must be a mistake. Es uno de los matices avanzados que distingue B2 de C1.

!
"That" es opcional, pero después del nombre no se omite

Tras say / tell / think, that es opcional: He said (that) he was tired. Pero tras explain, suggest, admit, deny, claim, announce, se prefiere escribirlo explícitamente: She explained that the project had been delayed.

Indirect Questions · Preguntas indirectas

Al reportar o suavizar una pregunta, esta deja de tener forma interrogativa: orden afirmativo (sujeto + verbo), sin auxiliar do/does/did y sin signo de interrogación al final. Las preguntas yes/no se introducen con if / whether; las wh- conservan su pronombre interrogativo.

🎯Linguaskill: aparece en Speaking (preguntas educadas en role-plays) y en Reading & Listening (transcripción de entrevistas, encuestas y noticias).
🔧

D. Trampas para hispanohablantes
Interferencias del español más frecuentes
!
NO inversión sujeto-verbo en la subordinada

La pregunta directa "Where does she live?" tiene inversión. La indirecta NO: He asked where did she live ✗ → He asked where she lived ✓. Mismo principio en Could you tell me where is the station? ✗ → Could you tell me where the station is? ✓.

!
NO auxiliar do / does / did en la subordinada

El auxiliar de pregunta desaparece: "Do you smoke?" → She asked if I smoked (no if I did smoke ✗). El tiempo verbal queda en la forma normal afirmativa, con backshift si toca.

!
"If" vs "whether" — ambos sirven, con matices

Para preguntas yes/no, ambos valen: I asked if / whether she was coming. Whether suena más formal y es obligatorio antes de to + infinitivo (I don't know whether to go or stay) y antes de or not al inicio (whether or not it rains). If nunca se usa en estos dos casos.

!
Sin signo de interrogación al final

La pregunta indirecta es una oración declarativa: termina en punto, no en "?": He asked where I lived. ✓ Pero ojo: si la oración completa es una pregunta (Could you tell me…?), entonces sí lleva "?": Could you tell me where the station is? El signo va por la oración principal, no por la subordinada.

!
"What" no es "qué" siempre — cuidado con la palabra interrogativa

Mantén el pronombre interrogativo original: "Why did she leave?" → He asked why she had left. No lo sustituyas por that: He asked that she had left ✗ (esto significaría "pidió que se fuera"). El wh- es obligatorio.

!
"Ask" + persona, no "ask to" + persona

"Le pregunté a Juan" no es I asked to John ✗ sino I asked John ✓. Ask funciona como tell: complemento directo de persona sin preposición. La preposición to solo aparece en I have a question to ask you.

Reporting Verbs & Patterns · Verbos de reporte y su patrón

Más allá de say y tell, en B2–C1 se espera variedad léxica: suggest, recommend, admit, deny, accuse, persuade, advise, warn, promise, offer, refuse, agree, apologise… Cada uno exige su propio patrón: + V-ing, + to + inf, + sb + to + inf, + sb + of + V-ing, + that + clause. Memorizar el patrón es lo que distingue una redacción mediocre de una alta.

🎯Linguaskill: imprescindible en Speaking Part 2.1 (resumir lo que alguien dijo), en Writing (reformular instrucciones u opiniones) y en Reading & Listening (huecos con reporting verbs).
⚠️Cuidado nº 1: suggest NO admite to + infinitivo. He suggested me to go ✗ → He suggested going ✓ o He suggested that I go / should go ✓. Mismo error con recommend: recommend me to try ✗ → recommend trying ✓.

E. En contexto · In context
Resúmenes estilo Speaking Part 2.1 — reporting language en acción

Model 1 · Resumen de una clase / Tutorial summary

Speaking Part 2.1 · ~95 words

Last Tuesday, the tutor explained that we would have to submit our research proposals before the end of the month. She recommended choosing a narrow topic and warned us not to underestimate the literature review. Several students complained about the tight deadline, but the tutor insisted that the dates were non-negotiable. At the end of the session, she offered to review a draft of our outline, and she reminded us that the next workshop would focus on referencing.

Model 2 · Resumen de una reunión / Meeting summary

Speaking Part 2.1 · ~90 words

In yesterday's team meeting, the manager announced that the company would launch a new product in September. She asked everyone to contribute ideas for the marketing campaign and suggested holding a brainstorming session next week. One colleague admitted that he hadn't read the latest report, while another apologised for missing the previous meeting. Finally, the manager promised to share the budget details by Friday and encouraged us to send any concerns by email.

Model 3 · Resumen de una conferencia / Lecture summary

Speaking Part 2.1 · ~95 words

The speaker, a well-known climate scientist, claimed that the next decade would be decisive for global emissions. He accused governments of failing to act on their commitments and criticised some industries for resisting regulation. However, he acknowledged that public awareness had improved significantly. He urged the audience to support local environmental initiatives and advised young professionals to consider careers in green sectors. At the end, he invited us to ask questions and offered to send his slides by email.

Model 4 · Resumen de una conversación con un cliente / Customer summary

Speaking Part 2.1 · ~90 words

Earlier today, a customer complained that her order had arrived two weeks late. She accused us of ignoring her previous emails and demanded that the company refund the full amount. I apologised for the inconvenience and assured her that we would investigate immediately. I also offered to send a replacement free of charge. She agreed to wait a few more days, but warned me that if the issue was not resolved she would post a negative review online.

Model 5 · Resumen de un debate televisivo / TV debate summary

Speaking Part 2.1 · ~95 words

During last night's debate, the two candidates disagreed about almost every issue. The first candidate accused his opponent of misleading voters and claimed that his policies would damage the economy. The second candidate denied lying and insisted that his proposals had been misrepresented. The moderator reminded them that personal attacks were not allowed and asked both speakers to focus on policy. Towards the end, both agreed to meet again before the elections to discuss healthcare in more detail.
